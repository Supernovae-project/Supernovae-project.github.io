Type I

Supernova are classified by the difference in the pattern of their luminosity over the course of the explosion. Type I supernovae have a sharp increase in their luminosity and then decrease in brightness "smoothly".Source Type I supernova are thought to occur when a white dwarf collapses from electron degeneracy pressure and explodes.Source


Type I supernova are broken into three categories based on existence of strong silicon, helium, or neither. No Type I supernova have hydrogen lines. The model that predicts this is based upon white dwarfs, which are born from low-mass to intermediate-mass stars and are carbon-dominant. If a white dwarf exists in a binary system it may have a strong enough gravity to pull matter from its companion and accrete it onto its surface. When this happens, its total mass increases until it reaches the Chandrasekhar limit of 1.44MSun. At this point, electron degeneracy pressure is unable to resist the dense mass in the white dwarf and it breaks down under the pressure of gravity. When this happens the white dwarf collapses and explodes in a massive eruption. The energy output of this explosion is very similar among observed Type Ia supernovae, ones that exhibit strong silicon spectral lines, and therefore they are more easily identified.Source

Type II

In high mass stars, those that are greater than 8MSun, the high core temperature allows for hydrogen to combine with carbon, oxygen, and nitrogen to produce helium. The heaviest element lower-mass stars can produce is carbon. This means that high mass stars can go through progressive stages of fusing increasingly heavy elements. This process will continue until iron begins to build up in a silicon-fusing core. Iron is special because it produces no energy when it undergoes nuclear fusion or fission. This is because these processes only creates energy when the product is lower mass than the combining molecules. Iron is the lowest mass element so fission and fusion will always produce more massive elements thus actually capturing energy not releasing it. When the core becomes iron, it will no longer radiate energy and resist gravitational force.Source


Electron degeneracy pressure, quantum-mechanics tells us that electrons will resist being close to one another, will keep the core from total collapse but once it crosses a certain limit, the core will collapse. This happens when the electrons can no longer resist each other and they combine with protons to form neutrons releasing neutrinos, cosmic particles, in the process. The crush of gravity now produces a core collapse that is only halted by neutron degeneracy pressure, leaving a ball of tightly-packed neutrons in a shrunken core. The neutrinos produced from the core collapse shoot outward and drive off the outer layers of the star in a massive release of energy, sending stellar matter into interstellar space. This produces a luminosity as powerful as 10 Billion Suns shining simultaneously. When the gas cloud of the former star begin to cool they will be visible as an expanding nebula.Source
